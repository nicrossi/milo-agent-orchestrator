# Activity: "The Weight of a Tree"

Goal: For the student to reconcile the origin of plant mass, logically deducing and explaining that a tree's physical weight comes primarily from invisible carbon dioxide in the air, not from consuming the solid soil.
Setup: You plant a tiny 1-pound seed in a large pot filled with exactly 100 pounds of dry soil. You water it for five years. The seed grows into a massive 50-pound tree! However, when you carefully weigh the dirt left in the pot, it still weighs 99.8 pounds. Where did the 49 pounds of heavy wood and leaves actually come from?

## 1. Descriptive | Aligned | Meaningful
M: The seed grew into a massive 50-pound tree, but the soil only lost 0.2 pounds. Where do you think the tree got the extra 49 pounds if it didn't come from the soil?
S: Well, it definitely didn't eat the dirt. The soil weight barely changed at all.
M: Great observation. If not the dirt, what else could have provided that much weight?
S: I read that plants do photosynthesis. It absorbed carbon dioxide from the air and used it to grow wood.
M: Exactly right! It built its mass from the air. Can you think of anything else that works similarly?
S: Yeah, it's kinda like a dry sponge. A plain sponge is super light, but if it soaks up unseen moisture from steam in a hot room, it gets heavy. The tree is just soaking up heavy gases instead of water.
